Before You Buy

Four Checks Before You Size

These factors move the sunroom sizing answer fastest.

01

Glass Exposure & Sun Load

South and west-facing glass absorbs the most heat. Sun direction is the biggest factor separating 12K from 18K.

02

3-Season vs 4-Season Use

Spring-and-fall use needs less capacity than year-round heating and cooling.

03

Insulation & Air Sealing

Thinner walls, single-pane glass, and air gaps push the right BTU higher.

04

Line-Set & Install Path

Confirm a clean wall for the indoor unit and a path for the line set to the outdoor condenser.

Quick Answers

Mini Split FAQ for Sunroom Buyers

What size mini split do I need for a sunroom?

Most attached sunrooms start with 12,000 BTU. Move to 18,000 BTU when the room has heavier glass exposure, stronger afternoon sun, or weaker insulation than a standard living space.

Is 12K BTU enough for a sunroom?

For many smaller and mid-size attached sunrooms with moderate glass, 12K is a solid starting point. If the room faces west, has floor-to-ceiling windows, or runs year-round, compare 18K before deciding.

Should I choose 18K BTU for a glass-heavy sunroom?

Glass-heavy sunrooms gain and lose heat faster than insulated rooms. If the room has large south or west-facing windows, weak insulation, or you want four-season comfort, 18K is worth comparing.

Can a mini split heat a sunroom in winter?

Yes. A heat-pump mini split handles both heating and cooling. For winter use, confirm the unit's cold-weather heating range and make sure the sunroom's insulation can support consistent operation.

What if my sunroom does not have enough wall space?

If floor-to-ceiling glass or sliding doors leave no clean wall-mount location, a ceiling cassette is the better option. It mounts flush in the ceiling and keeps the room looking clean. Choose BTU based on room size and glass exposure separately.
