Before You Buy

What to Check in an Older Home

Older homes have quirks. Confirm these four things before you order.

1

Electrical Panel

Most mini splits need a dedicated 115V or 230V circuit. Check if your panel has open breaker slots.

2

Insulation Quality

Better insulation means a smaller, quieter system. Uninsulated walls push the BTU requirement up.

3

Line-Set Route

Indoor and outdoor units connect through a 3" wall hole. Older walls and plaster may need a surface run.

4

Condenser Placement

The outdoor unit needs a flat surface with clearance. A side yard, patio, or wall bracket works.

Before You Ask

4 Questions Every Old-House Buyer Asks

01
Does my old wiring support it?
Most 100-amp panels handle a single-zone install. Panels under 100A may need an upgrade. Confirm with your electrician before ordering.
02
What about plaster or brick walls?
Only a 3-inch hole connects indoor and outdoor units. Plaster, brick, and stone walls all work. No large opening, no structural damage.
03
Will it actually heat in winter?
DELLA units operate down to −22°F outdoor temperature. No backup heater needed for most cold-climate regions in the continental US.
04
How long does the install take?
A typical single-zone job takes one day with two people. Each additional indoor unit adds half a day more. No major construction required.
Quick Answers

Mini Split FAQ for Old Houses

Common questions before you buy.

What mini split works best for an old house?

Start with room count. Most buyers begin with single-zone for one difficult room, then move to 2-zone or 3-zone if more rooms need help. Concealed ducted is the option when a cleaner look matters more.

Should I start with single-zone or go straight to 2-zone?

Start with single-zone if one room is clearly the main problem. Go straight to 2-zone when two rooms on the same floor both need regular heating and cooling with separate control.

When does 3-zone make sense?

When three connected rooms need independent comfort and you want one outdoor unit instead of stacking multiple systems. Usually best for larger upstairs or whole-floor retrofits.

When should I choose concealed ducted?

When you want less visible equipment and have a closet, attic, or ceiling cavity to hide the air handler. Wall-mounted is still the easier first option in most old houses.

Can I install without existing ductwork?

Yes. Single-zone, 2-zone, and 3-zone systems don't need traditional ductwork. Concealed ducted setups use short hidden runs when the layout calls for them.
