The most common answer is still wall-mounted. Cassette and floor-mounted options are useful, but they are not the default apartment starting point.

Most Common

Wall-Mounted

Ceiling Cassette

Before You Buy

What Apartment Buyers Should Check Before Choosing a Mini Split

Confirm landlord, HOA, condo, or building rules before assuming the outdoor unit placement is approved.
Check for a dedicated circuit and confirm whether the room can support 115V or 208-230V equipment cleanly.
Plan the line-set path and condensate drainage early. Apartment walls and exterior routes can narrow your install choices fast.
Do not oversize based only on the total apartment number. Closed rooms, poor insulation, top-floor heat, and window exposure can change the answer.
Start with wall-mounted unless you have a real layout reason to move to cassette or floor-mounted.
Common Questions

Mini Split FAQ for Apartment Shoppers

Quick answers before you buy.

What size mini split do I need for an apartment?

A small bedroom or office often lands at 9,000 BTU, most studios and main apartment living spaces start at 12,000 BTU, and larger open plans or hotter top-floor spaces often move to 18,000 BTU. Layout, insulation, and sun exposure matter as much as square footage.

Is a ductless mini split good for an apartment?

Yes. A ductless mini split is often a strong fit for apartment living because it targets one main room efficiently, runs quietly, and avoids the need for full ductwork. The main caveat is making sure your building allows the install path and outdoor unit placement.

What is the best indoor unit type for an apartment?

Wall-mounted is the most common indoor unit type for apartments. Ceiling cassette is the cleaner-look secondary option when the ceiling layout supports it, and floor-mounted is usually only used when upper wall space is limited.

Is 9K or 12K better for a studio apartment?

For a very compact studio, 9K can be enough. For many studio apartments, especially those with a combined living and sleeping zone, 12K is the more common starting point. That is why 12K is usually the safest default collection to compare first.

Can you install a mini split in an apartment building?

Often yes, but approval matters. You need to confirm exterior rules, condenser placement, line-set routing, condensate drainage, and electrical work before treating a product choice as final.

What should I check before buying a mini split for an apartment?

Check building approval, available electrical service, dedicated circuit requirements, the line-set route, drainage, and whether the room really needs 9K, 12K, or 18K. For apartment buyers, install practicality is part of the product decision.
